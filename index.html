Technology/Topic


A self-driving car is a vehicle that uses a combinations of cameras, sensors, radar and artificial intelligence (AI) to travel between destinations without a human operator.

Opportunities


Autonomous vehicles present an opportunity to provide an “inclusive design of new technology to accommodate to the full range of human diversity – from language, culture, gender, age, and other forms of human difference” (Baecker, 2019). According to ‘Computers in Society’, “the World Health Organisation (WHO) estimated that 246 million people have poor vision, 285 million people are visually impaired, and 39 million are blind”. Autonomous cars create an opportunity to provide a safe form of personal transport to not just visually impaired, but the general population that has a disability or those that have impairments to their driving abilities.


It was reported that the major causes for vehicular accidents were “Distracted driving, speeding, drink driving, reckless driving, rain, running red lights, and running stop signs in that order” (Baecker, 2019). Through the common belief that computational systems are more calculated and precise, automobile manufacturers saw an opportunity to develop smart vehicles to help mitigate and prevent these accidents. “Advances in sensor technology and machine learning were used to tackle technical challenges such as: (1) sensing the car’s location using GPS and 360-degree scanning lasers; (2) distinguishing sensed objects such as cars, cyclists, and pedestrians based on shape, movement pattern, and location; (3) tracking the locations and movements of these objects; (4) detecting unexpected and unknown objects that might affect what the car should do next; (5) anticipating atypical shapes and behaviors of these objects (such as kangaroos in Australia); (6) driving in a naturalistic manner, so as not to cause unsafe movements by other drivers; (7) substituting new conventions, such as inching forward at a four-way-stop intersection, as opposed to using eye contact.” (Baecker, 2019)




Risks


Autonomous vehicles also carry many risks associated with them as “designing safe autonomous vehicles are a challenge within themselves. Simulations and other forms of testing are used to find bugs but ultimately, the vehicles will need to be tested on the road to ensure there are no issues with the software and hardware of the autonomous vehicle”. There have been cases where autonomous vehicles malfunctioned. “The first was 2 cases from Uber as they were testing their autonomous driving features. Case 1 involved self-driving Uber cars driving through red lights in San-Francisco, and case 2 involved an accident where a driver failed to stop for an Uber vehicle in Arizona. The second case was a fatal accident in mid-2016, involving a Tesla vehicle operating in computer-assisted mode in Florida. After further investigation, it was found that the vehicle lacked the necessary safeguards to prevent the accident from occurring in the first place” (Baecker, 2019). While these are just 2 cases, it’s clear that there is still a risk to society with autonomous vehicles and their reliability towards public safety.

But AI wont be able to control the behavior of other drivers. With other drivers who speed, pass even when there is a double yellow line and drive the wrong way on a one- way street. Another choice that AI that could be a factor is weather conditions like snow, rain, fog, and any other type of weather that will make driving difficult for even humans to drive. What will AI do in these conditions, will the laser sensors be able to pick up the road with all snow or rain covering all the lines that help the vehicle stay in line.
